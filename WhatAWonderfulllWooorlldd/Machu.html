Machu Picchu

Built around 1450

Machu Picchu is an Incan citadel set high in the Andes Mountains in Peru, above the Urubamba River valley. Built in the 15th century and later abandoned, it’s renowned for its sophisticated dry-stone walls that fuse huge blocks without the use of mortar, intriguing buildings that play on astronomical alignments and panoramic views. Its exact former use remains a mystery.


Quick facts:

  • Located in the Andes Mountains of Peru, about 2,430 meters (7,970 ft) above sea level.
  • Believed to have been an estate for the Inca emperor Pachacuti or a ceremonial religious site
  • Although known locally, it was brought to international attention by American historian Hiram Bingham in 1911
  • Made from precisely cut stone blocks fitted together without mortar (a technique called ashlar masonry)
  • Situated in a tropical mountain forest, rich with biodiversity
  • "Machu Picchu" means “Old Mountain” in the Quechua language.
  • Considered one of the New Seven Wonders of the World as of 2007
  • Covers about 13 square kilometers (5 sq miles), with around 200 structures.
  • Believed to have been abandoned in the 16th century during the Spanish conquest, though the Spanish never found it
  • Most visitors arrive via the Inca Trail hike or by train to nearby Aguas Calientes
